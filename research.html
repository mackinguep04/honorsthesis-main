Research on Pretend Play & Gender Development

A comprehensive analysis of how gender influences children's pretend play, parental attitudes, and cultural differences in play behavior.

Explore the Research →

Research Overview

This research examines the complex relationships between gender, pretend play, parental involvement, and cultural contexts in early childhood development. Through analysis of existing studies, we explore how children's gender affects play behaviors, how parents' gender roles influence their engagement with children's imaginative activities, and how cultural backgrounds shape the nature and function of pretend play.

Gender Differences in Children's Play

Children's gender affects the type and frequency of pretend play, influencing parents' perceptions and involvement.

👧

Fantasy & Imagination

Girls were rated higher in their predilection for fantasy compared to boys by both mothers and fathers.

👦

Play Themes

Boys and girls engage in similar amounts of pretend play, but their themes differ, with girls favoring domestic roles and boys favoring adventurous themes.

👨‍👩‍👧

Parental Engagement

Parents are more likely to engage in pretend play with girls than with boys, reflecting gender biases in play.

Influence of Parents' Gender Roles

Mothers and fathers exhibit different attitudes and behaviors regarding their children's pretend play based on their gender roles.

✓

Mothers' Engagement: Mothers tend to engage more in pretend play and view it as beneficial for development, while fathers participate more spontaneously.

✓

Beliefs and Participation: The study found that mothers' beliefs about pretense were positively correlated with their participation, unlike fathers.

✓

Fathers' Concerns: Fathers reported more practical concerns regarding pretend play, such as setting limits compared to mothers.

Research Methodology and Participants

The study involved a diverse group of participants and utilized various methods to gather data on pretend play.

Key Findings and Statistical Analysis

The study's findings reveal significant differences in perceptions and attitudes based on imaginary companion status and child gender.

📊

Parent vs. Teacher Ratings

Parents rated children's involvement in pretend play higher than teachers did, with mothers' ratings significantly higher than fathers'.

🦄

Imaginary Companions

Children with imaginary companions received higher scores for involvement in pretense (M = 23.65) compared to those without (M = 21.82).

⚖️

Gender Differences

Mothers rating girls' pretend play (M = 23.70) higher than boys' (M = 21.30).

Parents' Attitudes Toward Pretend Play

Parents' beliefs about pretend play significantly influence their children's imaginative activities and development.

✓

Significance Ratings: Parents rated the significance of pretend play, their role in it, and when they would redirect children away from it.

✓

Imaginary Companion Status: No differences were found in composite ratings based on imaginary companion status for mothers and fathers.

✓

Mothers of Girls: Had more positive attitudes toward pretend play (M = 37.09) compared to mothers of boys (M = 33.55), p = .006.

✓

Fathers' Attitudes: Showed no differences in attitudes based on child gender.

✓

Language Development: Mothers who reported imaginary companions were more likely to endorse language development as a benefit of pretense (20% vs. 2.9%, p = .026).

Environment Provided for Pretend Play

The availability of toys and limits set by parents for pretend play varied based on child gender and imaginary companion status.

✓

Fantasy Toys for Daughters: Mothers and fathers reported providing more fantasy toys for daughters (M = 4.66 for mothers, M = 5.00 for fathers) than for sons (M = 4.05 for mothers, M = 4.00 for fathers).

✓

Fewer Limits: Fathers of children with imaginary companions reported placing fewer limits on pretense (M = 0.40) compared to those without (M = 1.29, p = .007).

✓

Guests and Pretense: A greater proportion of mothers of children with imaginary companions (29.6%) were likely to mind pretense when guests were over compared to those without (11.6%, p = .059).

✓

Family Dinners: Fathers of children with imaginary companions were less likely to mind pretense during family dinners (11.8% vs. 47.6%, p = .018).

Perceptions of Pretend Play by Parents

Parents' perceptions of children's pretend play were influenced by child gender and imaginary companion status.

👧

Daughters vs. Sons

A repeated-measures ANOVA showed that parents rated daughters' predilection for fantasy higher (M = 5.39) than sons' (M = 3.83, p < .001).

👩

Mothers vs. Fathers

Mothers held more positive views of imaginative play (M = 36.83) than fathers (M = 35.14, p = .03).

📝

Developmental Significance

No differences emerged in the number of categories cited by mothers and fathers regarding the developmental significance of pretend play.

Relation of Imaginary Companions to Parents' Views

Parents' attitudes toward pretend play were largely unaffected by whether their children had imaginary companions.

✓

Similar Ratings: Parents rated children's involvement in pretense similarly regardless of imaginary companion status.

✓

Language Development: Mothers of children with imaginary companions were more likely to associate pretend play with language development.

✓

Social Development: Fathers who reported imaginary companions were more likely to endorse social development benefits from pretense.

Child Gender's Impact on Parents' Views

Parents rated girls' pretend play higher than boys', despite research suggesting similar levels of pretend play across genders.

✓

Perceived Engagement: Mothers and fathers perceived daughters as more engaged in pretense than sons.

✓

Toy Provision: Parents provided more fantasy-oriented toys for daughters than sons, indicating gendered perceptions of play.

✓

Mealtime Limits: Fathers of sons were more likely to limit pretense during meals out (63.2% vs. 31.6% for daughters, p = .05).

Gender Differences in Parental Attitudes

Mothers exhibited more positive attitudes toward pretend play than fathers, suggesting differing roles in children's imaginative activities.

🧠

Cognitive Concerns

A higher proportion of mothers expressed concerns about cognitive issues related to pretend play compared to fathers.

⚠️

Worry Conditions

Mothers reported a wider variety of conditions under which they would worry about children's pretend play.

📏

Focus on Amount

Fathers were more focused on the amount of pretend play rather than its content, indicating a difference in perspective.

Gender Socialization

Process in which children learn the norms and social expectations for males and females in their culture (Ward, 2003).

  • Traditionally, children learned gender roles through observation of males and females in their families, later their friends and peers at school.
  • Media now provides early window on the larger worlds in front of a screen.
  • Their already established perception of gender may not be similar to what they consume in media.

Sex and Gender in Childhood

Between 2 and 7 years olds, most children come to understand that their biological sex is constant and does not change over time (Kohlberg, 1966).

Young children begin to establish patterns, or schemas, of beliefs and behaviors typically associated with biological sex differences prevalent in their culture.

✓

Gender Schemas: Use gender schemas to process and understand gender oriented behavior (Martin & Halverson, 1981), also influence memory and problem solving.

✓

Gender Stereotypes: Gender schemas lead to gender stereotypes because behavior that conforms to the established schema is more easily processed, understood, and remembered.

✓

Perception Example: Children with gender schema may see a female nurse and male doctor even though the video portrays otherwise (Cordua, McGraw, & Dragman, 1979).

How Gender Roles Are Learned

Gender roles are learned in three primary ways through gendered institutions.
